Bio

I'm not sure who cares enough to read this, but I go on other people's personal sites and find myself wanting to know more about what made them who they are, what makes them tick, and what gets them going—things that aren't found on a social media bio, a LinkedIn profile, or even in most interviews.

Rarely do people share their stories. This is my attempt at that.

Early Days

Born and raised in Manila, Philippines.

Extremely curious and always wondering about the future, I grew up drawing space rockets, cellular phones (back when there were hundreds of models in the market!), and architectural blueprints as a past time.

I went to Don Bosco Technical College to finish elementary school and start high school, studying information technology, mechanical & electrical engineering, and industrial drafting starting at the age of 10.

High School in America

My family moved to Yonkers, New York when I was 14. High school in Yonkers is where I grew my passion for the arts, dance, theater, and design. I was chosen as a candidate to take a summer intensive program at Columbia to take molecular biology but I failed the introduction assessment terribly. In despair, I started a hip-hop dance group with my friends instead that ended up winning the talent show. As a kid new to America but was in love with New York culture, to get this respect and recognition was way more fulfilling.

This was also when I started dabbling in web design heavily, designing and coding as many as 300+ MySpace layouts in a few years using mostly open source software, selling my services online to musicians, rappers, school clubs, and nonprofit orgs around the country. This helped finance my sneaker obsession at the time.

This was also where I largely grew my love for writing having studied 11th Grade English under Mr. Christopher Vicari, previously a National Teacher of the Year. This class expanded my horizons, imagination, and philosophical bent at a transformative phase in my life and I owe much of the development of my worldview thanks to this class and his tutelage. Many of my peers feel the same way to this day.

I also designed the class homecoming banner every year, was in student government 3 out of 4 years, won Best Actor for playing Macbeth (“Out, out, brief candle!”) and graduated with an International Baccalaureate diploma, one of the most rigorous high school curricula in the world. I was voted Most Talented and Most Creative (but ranked 3rd in Most Likely to Succeed, which I’m grateful for because it gave me a chip on my shoulder, which may or may not still exist to this day).

My best accomplishment in high school, however, was a successful guerrilla campaign using print & digital media to get all of the five public high schools in Yonkers to wear black for one day to protest for cleaner bathrooms. It was known as “PROJECT B14CK” (because it was held on the 14th of November) which I jumpstarted by disseminating posters around all five high schools with the help of the other student governments and set up a website discussing what it was about: a moment of solidarity for cleaner schools. It ended up being on the local TV station Channel 12 where students were interviewed about the issue.

College in Upstate New York

I later went to the University at Albany to study English and journalism with a focus on digital media. (Fun fact: I'm the youngest RA in the history of the school, having my own floor of fellow freshmen two months into college.)

Some of the most influential classes I took were: studying epics and the subject of “The Chosen One”, critical philosophy studying the Frankfurt School, urban planning through the lens of cities as a hidden character in cinema (which helped formulate thoughts on design and perspective), and identity through literature (One Hundred Years of Solitude and Song of Solomon were especially mindblowing). And little did I know, a class on reading and writing academic research was actually one I would use extensively post-college.

I became a member of an Asian-interest social fraternity which allowed me to have a leadership role on a national level immediately after I joined, something that was highly uncommon. I created the first national blog, updated the expansion packet, and later helped strategize and expand a new chapter at Rutgers University before I turned 21. I was also its first national public relations chair helping set the foundation of its initial online social presence and email marketing, boosting pride, membership, and the overall brand amongst Brothers and non-members alike.

Career in Technology

I was trying to establish a career in advertising when I came across Andrew Yang’s Smart People Should Build Things (long before he was a presidential candidate) and read it in a single sitting at the Barnes & Noble on E86th Street which convinced me to join startups. Soon after, I helped launch a mobile app for creative professionals for a startup called Bespoke, then joined a hiring startup named Apploi helping with its full redesign of both its consumer and enterprise applications for both web and mobile.

Afterwards, I had the opportunity to join Condé Nast—my actual dream company growing up as I wanted to write for The New Yorker and GQ—as they were building their digital team. With the help of a lot of talented designers, engineers, product managers, and literally some of the best editors in the world, I was able to work on numerous major projects. Some of the bigger ones include:

  • BRIDES redesign and wedding inspiration gallery,
  • Creating the initial proposal for a global design system,
  • Golf World redesign,
  • GQ Galleries,
  • GQ Best Stuff Newsletter (Digiday article)
  • Golf Digest Schools (the Masterclass for golf instruction),
  • Creating a framework for the distribution funnel of our owned and operated digital products,
  • Helping architect the universal design system that would power the entire company’s portfolio of brands, starting as the lead designer for homepages & channels, and later leading the pattern work for all of its components (Cheddar TV segment),
  • Leading the engineering team to build the front-end for AD Pro (premium membership publication for professional interior designers) using the aforementioned design system (WSJ feature)

The best part of working at Condé was: for some reason only the universe knows, David Remnick, editor of The New Yorker, became my mentor.

Theme

If there’s one central message of my life thus far, it would be this:

Dare to build new exciting things that didn’t exist before with your own style, voice, and conviction.

Be proud of your individuality, develop it internally, but most importantly, use it externally to liberate others and create new paths for progress. Creativity comes from having a strong perspective built from openness and having multiple sources for inspiration (mine are art, philosophy, business, architecture, fashion, music, literature). Use whatever suits you with the focus of a monk and the fury of a madman.

If it's not already obvious, I care about building products with great design, deep product thinking, and help push the realm of software forward.

Today

I am currently on sabbatical studying new disciplines, exploring new frontiers, and learning new technical skills in order to refine my beliefs and knowledge base.

I’m always looking for interesting people building the software of tomorrow. If that description fits you, feel free to reach out.
